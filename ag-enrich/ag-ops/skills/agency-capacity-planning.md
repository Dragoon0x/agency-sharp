# Agency Capacity Planning
**Purpose:** Apply agency capacity planning with the rigor and specificity that profitable agency delivery demands — where client expectations, fixed budgets, and quality standards leave no room for guesswork.

## Why This Matters

Agency operations is the business infrastructure that makes client delivery possible. Every operational decision — pricing model, team structure, tool selection, financial management — either creates leverage or creates drag. The best agencies operate like machines: predictable inputs produce consistent outputs.

For agency capacity planning specifically, the gap between doing this well and doing it poorly is the gap between a profitable project and a money-losing one. Agencies that systematize agency capacity planning deliver more consistently, scope more accurately, and retain clients longer than agencies that handle it ad-hoc.

## The Framework

Utilization Rate Management: Target 65-75% for creative roles (designers, strategists) and 75-85% for production roles (developers, QA). Below 60% utilization means the agency is losing money on that role. Above 85% means burnout is 6-8 weeks away. Track weekly, review monthly, adjust quarterly.

## How to Apply This

**Step 1: Assess the current state.** Before changing anything, document how agency capacity planning is currently handled. What works? What breaks? Where does the team spend unnecessary time? The assessment reveals whether the problem is process, people, tools, or all three.

**Step 2: Define the standard.** What does good look like for agency capacity planning in this agency, for this project type, at this scale? The standard should be specific enough that two team members would independently produce similar outputs. If the standard is vague, the results will be inconsistent.

**Step 3: Build the system.** Create the templates, checklists, documentation, and workflows that make the standard repeatable. The system should be lightweight enough to actually use and comprehensive enough to catch the common failure modes.

**Step 4: Measure and improve.** Track whether the system is working. The key metrics for agency capacity planning: Utilization rate (target: 70%), effective hourly rate (target: 2.5-3x employee cost), gross margin (target: 50-60%), client concentration (no client >25% of revenue), pipeline coverage (3x quarterly revenue target), employee satisfaction (quarterly pulse survey). Review quarterly. Adjust based on what the data shows, not what feels right.

## Real-World Use Cases

**Early-stage agency (1-5 people):** At this scale, agency capacity planning is often handled by the founder directly. The priority is documenting what works before the team grows. Create a simple version — a one-page checklist or template — that captures the founder's judgment so it can be delegated.

**Growth-stage agency (5-20 people):** At this scale, agency capacity planning must be delegated and systematized. The founder can't be in every loop. Build the standard, train the team, and create quality checks that catch problems without creating bottlenecks. The goal is consistent output without the founder's involvement in every instance.

**Scaled agency (20+ people):** At this scale, agency capacity planning needs governance — who owns the standard, how it's updated, how new team members are trained, and how exceptions are handled. The system should be self-maintaining: documented enough that a new PM or lead can manage it without institutional knowledge from the founders.

## Common Mistakes

**Hiring ahead of pipeline — adding headcount before the revenue to sustain it is proven.** This is the most common failure mode and the one that costs the most in rework, client friction, and margin erosion.

**Pricing by hours instead of value — hourly billing caps revenue at available hours.** This usually happens when the agency is moving too fast to follow the process — which is exactly when the process matters most.

**No financial visibility — not knowing utilization, margins, or runway until it's too late.** This compounds over time. Each instance is small. The cumulative effect is significant.

## The Agency-Specific Dimension

Agency work has constraints that product teams and freelancers don't face. Multiple clients means agency capacity planning must work across different brands, industries, and stakeholder dynamics. Parallel projects mean the team handles agency capacity planning for 3-5 clients simultaneously. Fixed budgets mean there's no room for the process to be inefficient. External deadlines mean the timeline is real. These constraints make systematizing agency capacity planning not optional but essential.

## Advanced Agency Operations

### The Agency Financial Dashboard
Track these 8 numbers monthly. If any is trending wrong for 2+ months, act immediately:

**1. Utilization Rate** — billable hours / available hours. Target: 70% blended (65-75% creative, 75-85% production). Below 60% = the agency is losing money on overhead. Above 85% = burnout is 6-8 weeks away. Track per person, per team, and blended. The blended number hides problems — a team at 90% and a team at 50% blend to 70%, which looks fine but isn't.

**2. Effective Rate** — actual revenue collected / actual hours worked. Not your rate card — what you actually earned per hour after scope creep, unbilled time, and write-offs. If effective rate is <2x fully loaded employee cost, the agency is subsidizing client work. Most agencies are shocked by this number the first time they calculate it honestly.

**3. Gross Margin** — (revenue - direct project costs) / revenue. Target: 50-60%. Direct costs = salaries of people working on the project + contractor costs + third-party tools billed to the project. Below 40% = pricing is too low or efficiency is too poor to sustain the business.

**4. Pipeline Coverage** — qualified pipeline value / quarterly revenue target. Target: 3-5x. Below 3x = revenue risk for next quarter. Above 5x = either great demand or poor qualification (inflated pipeline). Track separately: new business pipeline vs expansion/retainer pipeline.

**5. Revenue Concentration** — largest client as % of total revenue. Danger zone: any client >25%. If your largest client leaves, can the agency survive? Client concentration is the most common cause of agency death. Diversify deliberately: set a maximum client concentration threshold and decline work that would exceed it.

**6. Employee NPS** — quarterly anonymous survey: 'On a scale of 0-10, how likely are you to recommend working here?' Below 30 = retention crisis incoming. Above 50 = healthy culture. Track by team, tenure, and role. The segments reveal problems the average hides.

**7. Win Rate** — proposals won / proposals submitted. Healthy: 25-40%. Below 20% = proposals are weak or leads are unqualified. Above 50% = likely underpricing. Track by project type, client industry, and deal size to identify patterns.

**8. Average Project Margin** — post-project actual profit / revenue. Target: 40-50% after direct costs. Track per project and look for patterns: which project types are most/least profitable? Which clients? Which teams? The patterns reveal where to focus and where to fix.

### Case Study: How Ueno Scaled to $20M
Ueno (now part of Twitter) grew from a 2-person Icelandic design studio to a $20M multi-office agency by following three principles: (1) Productize the pitch — every proposal followed the same structure, reducing pitch creation from 40 hours to 8. (2) Pod model — each pod (strategist + designer + developer) owned client relationships end-to-end, eliminating handoff friction. (3) Radical transparency — every employee could see every project's budget, timeline, and margin, which created collective ownership of profitability.

### The Agency Operating Rhythm
**Daily:** Team standup (15 min max, async if remote). PMs check project health dashboards.
**Weekly:** Monday — project pipeline review (what's at risk). Friday — status updates to all active clients (same format, every week, no exceptions).
**Monthly:** Financial review (the 8 numbers above). Resource planning (who's available, who's overloaded, what's coming). Team retrospective (what's working, what's not, what to change).
**Quarterly:** Strategic review (positioning, services, pricing, team structure). Client health review (NPS scores, growth opportunities, at-risk relationships). Rate review (should we adjust pricing for new work?).
**Annually:** Strategic planning (where are we going next year). Compensation review. Brand refresh assessment. Tool stack audit.

The rhythm creates predictability. When everyone knows what happens when, the operational overhead of coordination drops dramatically. The rhythm is the operating system.





### The Agency Positioning Decision Tree
**Decision 1: Generalist or specialist?** If under $1M revenue → specialize (concentration creates expertise and reputation faster). If over $3M → consider controlled expansion into adjacent specialties.

**Decision 2: Service specialist or industry specialist?** Service specialist (we do branding for everyone) creates depth. Industry specialist (we do everything for healthcare) creates breadth within a niche. The strongest position: service + industry ("we do brand strategy for B2B SaaS companies").

**Decision 3: Premium or accessible?** Premium positioning (higher rates, fewer clients, deeper work) requires: distinctive creative quality, proven results, and the willingness to say no to budget-constrained prospects. Accessible positioning (competitive rates, more clients, efficient delivery) requires: productized services, streamlined processes, and scale efficiency.

### The First 5 Hires (In Order)
1. **Producer/PM** — frees the founder from day-to-day project management. The founder can now sell and do strategy.
2. **Senior designer or developer** (whichever is the core delivery skill) — someone who can own delivery quality without founder review on every detail.
3. **Junior in the core skill** — leverage the senior hire's capacity with a less expensive resource for production work.
4. **Operations/finance person** (part-time/fractional) — invoicing, bookkeeping, contracts, and the administrative work the founder has been doing at midnight.
5. **Second senior in the complementary skill** — if the first senior is design, the second is development (or vice versa). Now the agency has a complete delivery capability without the founder.

Each hire should be validated by: can we keep this person utilized at 70%+ for the next 6 months based on current pipeline? If the answer is "probably," use a contractor first to test the demand before committing to a full-time hire.


## The Agency Practitioner's Playbook

### Patterns That Recur Across Every Agency Domain

**Pattern 1: Process investment pays 10x.** Every hour invested in documenting and systematizing how the agency works saves 10 hours of ad-hoc problem-solving, onboarding confusion, and inconsistent quality. Process isn't bureaucracy — it's the operating system. The agencies that resist process stay small and stressed. The agencies that embrace it scale with sanity.

**Pattern 2: Client communication prevents 80% of problems.** The vast majority of client conflicts — about scope, quality, timeline, budget — trace back to communication gaps. A weekly status update in a consistent format prevents more client escalations than any amount of excellent work delivered silently. Overcommunication is almost never the problem. Undercommunication almost always is.

**Pattern 3: Quality costs less than rework.** Spending an extra 10% on quality (design review, code review, pre-launch checklist, content QA) prevents 50%+ of post-launch fixes, client complaints, and emergency patches. The math always favors investing in prevention over paying for repair. Yet most agencies underinvest in quality because the cost is visible and immediate while the savings are invisible and distributed.

**Pattern 4: Specialization beats generalism.** Agencies that specialize (by industry, by service, by technology, by company stage) charge more, win more, and deliver better than agencies that do everything for everyone. Specialization creates expertise, expertise creates efficiency, efficiency creates margin, and margin creates sustainability. The fear: "But we'll lose clients who need other things." The reality: you'll lose bad-fit clients and attract good-fit clients at higher rates.

### The Career Progression in Agency Work

**Junior (0-2 years):** Learn the craft. Execute tasks within a defined process. Develop skills in your discipline. Focus: quality of output.

**Mid (2-5 years):** Own deliverables end-to-end. Manage components of client relationships. Begin mentoring juniors. Focus: quality of process.

**Senior (5-8 years):** Own client relationships. Lead teams on complex projects. Contribute to agency strategy. Focus: quality of judgment.

**Lead/Director (8+ years):** Set standards for the discipline. Win and manage key accounts. Shape agency direction. Mentor seniors. Focus: quality of the system.

**Partner/Principal (10+ years):** Drive agency growth. Own the agency's market position. Build the team's capability. Focus: quality of the agency itself.

At every level, the shift is from doing the work to improving how the work gets done. The most valuable agency people aren't the best individual contributors — they're the people who make everyone else better.

### Mental Models for Agency Thinking

**The Service-Profit Chain:** Happy employees → better work → happy clients → referrals and retention → revenue → investment in employees → happier employees. The chain starts with employee experience, not client experience. Agencies that treat employees as fungible resources break the chain at the first link.

**The Trust Equation (Maister):** Trust = (Credibility + Reliability + Intimacy) / Self-Orientation. Credibility: do you know your stuff? Reliability: do you deliver what you promise? Intimacy: does the client feel safe with you? Self-Orientation: are you focused on the client's interests or your own? The denominator matters most — self-oriented agencies destroy trust regardless of how credible, reliable, or intimate they are.

**The Hedgehog Concept (Collins):** The intersection of: (1) What you're deeply passionate about, (2) What you can be best in the world at, and (3) What drives your economic engine. For agencies: passionate about a specific craft, best at a specific service for a specific audience, with an economic model that sustains the team. Agencies that operate outside the intersection are perpetually struggling. Agencies that find it are sustainable and growing.

### Metrics That Matter Across All Domains

Track these 5 meta-metrics regardless of your specific role:

1. **Client satisfaction trend** — are clients happier or less happy over the last 3 quarters?
2. **Team satisfaction trend** — is the team more or less engaged over the last 3 quarters?
3. **Revenue per employee** — is the agency getting more efficient or less?
4. **Repeat business rate** — are clients coming back? (Target: 60%+ of revenue from existing clients)
5. **Referral rate** — are clients sending new clients? (Target: 30%+ of new business from referrals)

If all 5 are trending positively, the agency is healthy regardless of what individual project metrics show. If any is trending negatively for 2+ quarters, there's a systemic issue that needs diagnosis.
