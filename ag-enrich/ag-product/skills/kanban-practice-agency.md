# Kanban Practice Agency
**Purpose:** Apply kanban practice agency with the rigor and specificity that profitable agency delivery demands — where client expectations, fixed budgets, and quality standards leave no room for guesswork.

## Why This Matters

Product management in an agency context means ensuring that what gets built actually solves the client's user's problem — not just what the client asked for. The agency PM bridges client business objectives, user needs, and technical feasibility, making tradeoff decisions that maximize value within budget and timeline constraints.

For kanban practice agency specifically, the gap between doing this well and doing it poorly is the gap between a profitable project and a money-losing one. Agencies that systematize kanban practice agency deliver more consistently, scope more accurately, and retain clients longer than agencies that handle it ad-hoc.

## The Framework

Prioritization for Agency Work: Impact (how much does this move the client's key metric?) × Confidence (how sure are we?) / Effort (person-days). But add an agency-specific filter: Client Priority (is this what the client cares about most?). A high-impact, low-effort feature that the client doesn't care about is politically useless.

## How to Apply This

**Step 1: Assess the current state.** Before changing anything, document how kanban practice agency is currently handled. What works? What breaks? Where does the team spend unnecessary time? The assessment reveals whether the problem is process, people, tools, or all three.

**Step 2: Define the standard.** What does good look like for kanban practice agency in this agency, for this project type, at this scale? The standard should be specific enough that two team members would independently produce similar outputs. If the standard is vague, the results will be inconsistent.

**Step 3: Build the system.** Create the templates, checklists, documentation, and workflows that make the standard repeatable. The system should be lightweight enough to actually use and comprehensive enough to catch the common failure modes.

**Step 4: Measure and improve.** Track whether the system is working. The key metrics for kanban practice agency: Sprint velocity (stories completed per sprint), scope change rate, client satisfaction per sprint, user metric improvement post-launch, time-to-launch vs planned, defect escape rate. Review quarterly. Adjust based on what the data shows, not what feels right.

## Real-World Use Cases

**Early-stage agency (1-5 people):** At this scale, kanban practice agency is often handled by the founder directly. The priority is documenting what works before the team grows. Create a simple version — a one-page checklist or template — that captures the founder's judgment so it can be delegated.

**Growth-stage agency (5-20 people):** At this scale, kanban practice agency must be delegated and systematized. The founder can't be in every loop. Build the standard, train the team, and create quality checks that catch problems without creating bottlenecks. The goal is consistent output without the founder's involvement in every instance.

**Scaled agency (20+ people):** At this scale, kanban practice agency needs governance — who owns the standard, how it's updated, how new team members are trained, and how exceptions are handled. The system should be self-maintaining: documented enough that a new PM or lead can manage it without institutional knowledge from the founders.

## Common Mistakes

**No success metrics defined before building — no way to know if the product worked.** This is the most common failure mode and the one that costs the most in rework, client friction, and margin erosion.

**Scope creep through 'small' additions — features that are 'just one more thing' that collectively double the project.** This usually happens when the agency is moving too fast to follow the process — which is exactly when the process matters most.

**Not showing work in progress — surprising the client at the end instead of aligning throughout.** This compounds over time. Each instance is small. The cumulative effect is significant.

## The Agency-Specific Dimension

Agency work has constraints that product teams and freelancers don't face. Multiple clients means kanban practice agency must work across different brands, industries, and stakeholder dynamics. Parallel projects mean the team handles kanban practice agency for 3-5 clients simultaneously. Fixed budgets mean there's no room for the process to be inefficient. External deadlines mean the timeline is real. These constraints make systematizing kanban practice agency not optional but essential.

## Advanced Product Craft for Agencies

### The Agency PM's Unique Challenges
Product management in an agency is fundamentally different from product management in a product company:

**You don't own the product.** The client does. Your recommendations are recommendations, not decisions. The skill is building enough trust that your recommendations are followed — and knowing when to defer to the client's domain expertise.

**The timeline is fixed.** Product companies can extend timelines. Agency projects have contracted deadlines. The PM's job is scope management — fitting the right scope into the available time, not extending the time to fit the desired scope.

**Multiple products simultaneously.** An agency PM might manage 3-5 client products at once. Context-switching is the enemy. The fix: time-block each client (Mondays = Client A, Tuesdays = Client B) rather than interleaving throughout the day.

**No long-term iteration.** Product companies iterate for years. Agency projects last weeks or months. The PM must make strategic bets that pay off quickly — there's no luxury of "we'll learn and adjust over 6 quarters." Discovery must be compressed. Validation must be fast. Shipping must be on the first attempt.

### The Strategic Product Recommendation
Agencies often need to recommend product strategy, not just execute features. The recommendation framework:

**Evidence section:** "Based on [12 user interviews / analytics data / competitive analysis], we observed [specific pattern]." Ground every recommendation in evidence, not opinion.

**Insight section:** "This pattern suggests [interpretation]." The interpretation is where the PM adds value — connecting observations to strategic implications.

**Recommendation section:** "We recommend [specific action] because [reasoning tied to the insight]." The recommendation must be actionable — not "improve the onboarding" but "reduce the onboarding from 7 steps to 3, focusing on [specific steps to keep]."

**Trade-off section:** "This recommendation prioritizes [X] over [Y]. The risk is [specific risk]. We mitigate this by [specific mitigation]." Showing the trade-offs builds trust — the client knows you've thought about the downsides, not just the upsides.





### The Product Recommendation Framework for Agencies
When an agency advises on product decisions:

**The Evidence-Based Recommendation:** Every recommendation must be grounded in evidence, not opinion. Structure: "Based on [evidence type: user research / analytics / competitive analysis / industry benchmark], we observed [specific finding]. This suggests [interpretation]. We recommend [specific action] because [reasoning]. The expected impact is [measurable outcome]. The risk is [specific risk] which we mitigate by [mitigation]."

**The Options Presentation:** Never present one option. Present 2-3 options with trade-offs clearly stated. Option A: maximum impact, maximum effort. Option B: moderate impact, moderate effort (usually the recommended option). Option C: minimum viable, minimum effort. The client chooses. This structure: respects the client's decision authority, demonstrates strategic thinking (you've considered multiple approaches), and creates a natural upsell opportunity (clients often choose B when they were expecting C).

**The Validation Sprint:** For high-stakes product recommendations, propose a 1-2 week validation sprint before full execution. The sprint tests the riskiest assumption cheaply — a prototype, a landing page test, a survey, or a concierge MVP. The sprint costs $5K-$15K and can prevent a $100K mistake. Position it as risk reduction, not delay.

**The Success Metric Contract:** Before execution begins, agree with the client on: what metric will determine success, what the target is, and when it will be measured. Write it down. Review it at project completion. This prevents the post-launch debate about whether the project "worked" — the definition of success was agreed before the work started.


## The Agency Practitioner's Playbook

### Patterns That Recur Across Every Agency Domain

**Pattern 1: Process investment pays 10x.** Every hour invested in documenting and systematizing how the agency works saves 10 hours of ad-hoc problem-solving, onboarding confusion, and inconsistent quality. Process isn't bureaucracy — it's the operating system. The agencies that resist process stay small and stressed. The agencies that embrace it scale with sanity.

**Pattern 2: Client communication prevents 80% of problems.** The vast majority of client conflicts — about scope, quality, timeline, budget — trace back to communication gaps. A weekly status update in a consistent format prevents more client escalations than any amount of excellent work delivered silently. Overcommunication is almost never the problem. Undercommunication almost always is.

**Pattern 3: Quality costs less than rework.** Spending an extra 10% on quality (design review, code review, pre-launch checklist, content QA) prevents 50%+ of post-launch fixes, client complaints, and emergency patches. The math always favors investing in prevention over paying for repair. Yet most agencies underinvest in quality because the cost is visible and immediate while the savings are invisible and distributed.

**Pattern 4: Specialization beats generalism.** Agencies that specialize (by industry, by service, by technology, by company stage) charge more, win more, and deliver better than agencies that do everything for everyone. Specialization creates expertise, expertise creates efficiency, efficiency creates margin, and margin creates sustainability. The fear: "But we'll lose clients who need other things." The reality: you'll lose bad-fit clients and attract good-fit clients at higher rates.

### The Career Progression in Agency Work

**Junior (0-2 years):** Learn the craft. Execute tasks within a defined process. Develop skills in your discipline. Focus: quality of output.

**Mid (2-5 years):** Own deliverables end-to-end. Manage components of client relationships. Begin mentoring juniors. Focus: quality of process.

**Senior (5-8 years):** Own client relationships. Lead teams on complex projects. Contribute to agency strategy. Focus: quality of judgment.

**Lead/Director (8+ years):** Set standards for the discipline. Win and manage key accounts. Shape agency direction. Mentor seniors. Focus: quality of the system.

**Partner/Principal (10+ years):** Drive agency growth. Own the agency's market position. Build the team's capability. Focus: quality of the agency itself.

At every level, the shift is from doing the work to improving how the work gets done. The most valuable agency people aren't the best individual contributors — they're the people who make everyone else better.

### Mental Models for Agency Thinking

**The Service-Profit Chain:** Happy employees → better work → happy clients → referrals and retention → revenue → investment in employees → happier employees. The chain starts with employee experience, not client experience. Agencies that treat employees as fungible resources break the chain at the first link.

**The Trust Equation (Maister):** Trust = (Credibility + Reliability + Intimacy) / Self-Orientation. Credibility: do you know your stuff? Reliability: do you deliver what you promise? Intimacy: does the client feel safe with you? Self-Orientation: are you focused on the client's interests or your own? The denominator matters most — self-oriented agencies destroy trust regardless of how credible, reliable, or intimate they are.

**The Hedgehog Concept (Collins):** The intersection of: (1) What you're deeply passionate about, (2) What you can be best in the world at, and (3) What drives your economic engine. For agencies: passionate about a specific craft, best at a specific service for a specific audience, with an economic model that sustains the team. Agencies that operate outside the intersection are perpetually struggling. Agencies that find it are sustainable and growing.

### Metrics That Matter Across All Domains

Track these 5 meta-metrics regardless of your specific role:

1. **Client satisfaction trend** — are clients happier or less happy over the last 3 quarters?
2. **Team satisfaction trend** — is the team more or less engaged over the last 3 quarters?
3. **Revenue per employee** — is the agency getting more efficient or less?
4. **Repeat business rate** — are clients coming back? (Target: 60%+ of revenue from existing clients)
5. **Referral rate** — are clients sending new clients? (Target: 30%+ of new business from referrals)

If all 5 are trending positively, the agency is healthy regardless of what individual project metrics show. If any is trending negatively for 2+ quarters, there's a systemic issue that needs diagnosis.
