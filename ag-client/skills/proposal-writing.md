# Proposal Writing
**Purpose:** Write proposals that win work and set engagements up for success — not generic pitch decks but specific, persuasive documents that demonstrate understanding of the client's problem.

A winning proposal does three things: it proves you understand the client's problem better than they do, it presents a specific plan to solve it, and it makes the investment feel like an obvious decision. Most proposals fail on the first point — they describe what the agency does instead of what the client needs.

## The Proposal Structure
Executive summary (3 sentences: the problem, the solution, the outcome). Understanding of the challenge (prove you've listened — reflect their situation back to them in more precise language than they used). Approach (how you'll solve it — not generic process but specific to their situation). Deliverables (exactly what they get, when). Timeline (phase by phase with milestones). Investment (the price, positioned after the value). Team (who will do the work — names, roles, relevant experience). Next steps (make the action clear).

## The Pricing Frame
Never present price in isolation. Always present it after the value section. If the project will increase conversion by 25% on $2M annual revenue, the ROI frame is: "$500K in additional annual revenue, for a one-time investment of $85K." The price becomes a fraction of the return instead of a cost to be minimized.

## The Win Rate Diagnostic
If you're winning less than 30% of proposals, either the leads are unqualified (filter harder) or the proposals are weak (improve the problem-understanding section). If you're winning more than 70%, you're probably underpricing.

## When to use
Every new business opportunity. When win rates need improvement. When average deal size needs to increase. When proposals take too long to produce.
