# Wip Management
**Purpose:** Apply wip management with the rigor and specificity that profitable agency delivery demands — where client expectations, fixed budgets, and quality standards leave no room for guesswork.

## Why This Matters

Agency project management is coordination under constraint — multiple clients, parallel projects, shared resources, fixed budgets, external deadlines, and the constant tension between quality and speed. The PM's job is not to do the work but to create the conditions where the team can do their best work on time and on budget.

For wip management specifically, the gap between doing this well and doing it poorly is the gap between a profitable project and a money-losing one. Agencies that systematize wip management deliver more consistently, scope more accurately, and retain clients longer than agencies that handle it ad-hoc.

## The Framework

The Status Update Format: Project name. Period. Progress (what was completed — be specific). Next period (what's planned). Decisions needed (anything blocking — with deadline). Risks (anything threatening timeline or budget). Budget status (hours spent vs allocated). This takes 15 minutes to write. Send weekly, same day, same format.

## How to Apply This

**Step 1: Assess the current state.** Before changing anything, document how wip management is currently handled. What works? What breaks? Where does the team spend unnecessary time? The assessment reveals whether the problem is process, people, tools, or all three.

**Step 2: Define the standard.** What does good look like for wip management in this agency, for this project type, at this scale? The standard should be specific enough that two team members would independently produce similar outputs. If the standard is vague, the results will be inconsistent.

**Step 3: Build the system.** Create the templates, checklists, documentation, and workflows that make the standard repeatable. The system should be lightweight enough to actually use and comprehensive enough to catch the common failure modes.

**Step 4: Measure and improve.** Track whether the system is working. The key metrics for wip management: On-time delivery rate (target: 85%+), on-budget delivery rate (target: 80%+), scope change frequency, client satisfaction score, team satisfaction score, lessons-learned implementation rate. Review quarterly. Adjust based on what the data shows, not what feels right.

## Real-World Use Cases

**Early-stage agency (1-5 people):** At this scale, wip management is often handled by the founder directly. The priority is documenting what works before the team grows. Create a simple version — a one-page checklist or template — that captures the founder's judgment so it can be delegated.

**Growth-stage agency (5-20 people):** At this scale, wip management must be delegated and systematized. The founder can't be in every loop. Build the standard, train the team, and create quality checks that catch problems without creating bottlenecks. The goal is consistent output without the founder's involvement in every instance.

**Scaled agency (20+ people):** At this scale, wip management needs governance — who owns the standard, how it's updated, how new team members are trained, and how exceptions are handled. The system should be self-maintaining: documented enough that a new PM or lead can manage it without institutional knowledge from the founders.

## Common Mistakes

**No risk management — treating every project as if nothing will go wrong.** This is the most common failure mode and the one that costs the most in rework, client friction, and margin erosion.

**Retrospectives that blame people instead of improving systems.** This usually happens when the agency is moving too fast to follow the process — which is exactly when the process matters most.

**No project health visibility until it's too late — not tracking budget and timeline continuously.** This compounds over time. Each instance is small. The cumulative effect is significant.

## The Agency-Specific Dimension

Agency work has constraints that product teams and freelancers don't face. Multiple clients means wip management must work across different brands, industries, and stakeholder dynamics. Parallel projects mean the team handles wip management for 3-5 clients simultaneously. Fixed budgets mean there's no room for the process to be inefficient. External deadlines mean the timeline is real. These constraints make systematizing wip management not optional but essential.

## Metrics to Track

On-time delivery rate (target: 85%+), on-budget delivery rate (target: 80%+), scope change frequency, client satisfaction score, team satisfaction score, lessons-learned implementation rate

## When to use

When wip management is the current bottleneck in delivery or operations. When quality in this area is inconsistent across projects or team members. When the agency is scaling and needs wip management to work without the founder in the loop. When post-project retrospectives identify this as a recurring issue.
