# SOW Design
**Purpose:** Write statements of work that are clear enough to prevent disputes, flexible enough to accommodate reality, and specific enough that both parties can independently determine whether the work is complete.

The SOW is the single most important document in any agency engagement. It defines what you'll deliver, when, for how much, and what happens when things change. A weak SOW is an invitation for scope creep, payment disputes, and relationship damage. A strong SOW protects both parties and makes the working relationship smoother.

## The SOW Structure
Project overview (2-3 sentences — what we're doing and why). Scope of work (specific deliverables, numbered, with enough detail to be unambiguous). Out of scope (explicitly list what's NOT included — this is as important as what IS included). Timeline (phases with milestones and dates). Payment schedule (tied to milestones, not dates). Revision policy (number of rounds included, cost of additional rounds). Change order process (how scope changes are requested, assessed, and approved). Acceptance criteria (how the client confirms deliverables are complete).

## The Specificity Standard
Bad: "Website design and development." Good: "Design and development of a 6-page responsive website (Home, About, Services, Portfolio, Blog index, Contact) including mobile and desktop breakpoints, built on Next.js with headless CMS integration (Sanity), including 2 rounds of design revisions per page and 1 round of development QA."

## The Protection Clauses
Kill fee: if the client cancels mid-project, they pay for work completed plus 20-25% of remaining scope. Payment terms: net-15 or net-30, with late fees. IP transfer: IP transfers to client upon final payment (not before — this is your leverage for payment). Liability: limited to the value of the contract.

## When to use
Every client engagement. When standardizing the SOW process. When past projects have had scope disputes. When projects aren't getting paid on time.
