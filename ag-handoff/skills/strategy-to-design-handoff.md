# Strategy-to-Design Handoff
**Purpose:** Hand off strategy to design without losing insight — the transition where research findings, strategic decisions, and client alignment must be compressed into a brief that design can execute from.

The strategy-to-design handoff is where most agency projects lose quality. The strategist understands the client, the user, and the competitive landscape. The designer needs to translate all of that into visual decisions. The handoff brief is the bridge — and when it's weak, the design doesn't match the strategy.

## The Handoff Deliverable
The design brief should include: positioning summary (who are we, for whom, and why are we different), audience definition (who are we designing for, what do they care about, what language do they use), competitive visual landscape (screenshots + analysis of what competitors look like), design principles (3-5 specific principles that guide visual decisions), moodboard or visual territory (the aesthetic direction, with rationale), and specific requirements (pages needed, content hierarchy, technical constraints).

## The Handoff Meeting
A written brief is necessary but not sufficient. The handoff meeting lets the designer ask questions, challenge assumptions, and clarify ambiguity. The meeting should be 60-90 minutes, facilitated by the strategist, and result in the designer confirming they have everything needed to start exploring.

## The Feedback Loop
The first design presentation should be evaluated against the strategy, not against personal preference. The question is not "do I like this?" but "does this execute the strategy?" If the strategy said "premium and minimal" and the design is playful and dense, the design doesn't match — regardless of whether it looks good.

## When to use
Every project that has a strategy phase before a design phase. When designs aren't matching the strategic direction. When designers complain about unclear briefs.
